# 2. Description of the ESMa

## Observed reasoning pattern

Some students develop an operational understanding of the equal sign,
interpreting it mainly as a signal indicating that they must perform an
operation and provide a final answer.

In this interpretation, the equal sign functions as a separator between
a mathematical expression and its result, instead of being understood as
a relational symbol establishing that two expressions represent the same
mathematical value.

This conception may be functional for solving simple arithmetic
operations where the result appears after the equal sign, but it becomes
a limitation when students encounter open expressions, equations, or
algebraic situations where both sides of the equal sign contain relevant
mathematical information.

## Observable manifestation

Example of written mathematical work produced by a first-semester high
school student:

Expression:

5/6 - 1/3 + 1/2

The student begins the procedure:

5/6 - 1/3 =

and obtains:

5/6 - 1/3 = (5 x 2)/6 =

The student then continues without preserving the complete structure of
the original expression, showing a fragmented interpretation of the
mathematical process:

5/6 - 1/3 = (5 x 2)/6 = 10/6 + 1/2 = (10 x 3)/6 = 30/6

## Expected mathematical thinking

The complete expression should be preserved as a mathematical unit until
all operations have been completed.

5/6 - 1/3 + 1/2

Using a common denominator:

5/6 - 1/3 + 1/2 = 5/6 - 2/6 + 3/6 = 6/6 = 1

The equal sign represents equivalence between expressions, not merely an
instruction to obtain an answer.

# 4. Cognitive Analysis

## Observable thinking

The student appears to treat each intermediate transformation as a
partial conclusion instead of maintaining the complete expression as a
continuous mathematical relationship.

## Possible mental model

The student may have constructed the implicit rule:

> After the equal sign, an answer must appear.

This mental model frequently emerges from early experiences where
exercises such as:

3+4 = \[ \]

reinforce the association between the equal sign and obtaining a final
result.

# 5. Origin and Related Prior Knowledge

## Possible cognitive origin

During the first years of mathematical learning, many students primarily
work with expressions such as:

a+b=c

where the right side represents an answer.

Although this interpretation works in initial contexts, it can later
become an obstacle to understanding equivalent expressions, equations,
and algebraic structures.

## Related prior knowledge

-   Understanding the equal sign as a calculation operator.
-   Mechanical resolution of operations.
-   Limited exposure to equivalent expressions.
-   Limited experience with early equations as relationships between
    quantities.

# 6. Scientific Evidence and Bibliography

## Related research

equal sign as an operational signal equivalent to "and the answer is",

# 7. Educational Intervention

## Suggested teaching strategies

-   Present expressions where the equal sign appears between two
    equivalent expressions.
-   Work with open equations such as:

8+4 = \[ \]

-   Use visual representations of balance and equivalence.
-   Promote explanations of reasoning, not only procedures.

## Recommended teacher questions

-   What does the equal sign really mean in this expression?
-   Do the values before and after the equal sign represent the same
    quantity?
-   Could we write another expression with the same value?
-   Does the equal sign always mean "the answer comes here"?

## Useful representations or resources

-   Mathematical balance models.
-   Equivalent expression cards.
-   Graphic representations.
-   Educational narratives.
